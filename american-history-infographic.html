How America Gave Up

Unable to agree on how to interpret the American story, the country's schools, universities, and political institutions have stopped trying to tell it at all.

01 · The Core Crisis

A Nation Built on an Idea — Losing Its Story

In 2019, Appelbaum joins a blue-ribbon commission convened to find America's shared narrative. When he says the word "patriotism" as common ground, the room nearly explodes. The scene defines the whole essay.

A nation defined by blood and soil can survive divergent narratives. But to a country built on an idea and bound together by a shared understanding of history, the inability to tell a common story might well prove fatal.

— Yoni Appelbaum, The Atlantic, July 2026
Core Thesis

The United States — unlike nations built on ethnicity or religion — holds itself together through a shared creedal narrative: a commitment to equality, rights, and democratic ideals. When Americans cannot agree on how to tell that story, they do not just argue — they risk losing the very bond that makes them a nation. And increasingly, they have stopped trying to tell the story at all.

02 · The History Wars

Three Competing Schools — One Dangerous Convergence

Historian Johann Neem maps the fracture into three competing visions of the American story. Each has institutional homes, political champions, and real consequences for education and culture.

Irredeemably Broken

The Mainstream School
Work in Progress

The Hyper-American Right
Providential Origin

America's founding was near-immaculate. Slavery was a deviation from — not a foundation of — its ideals. Too much darkness risks disenchanting the nation. Approach history "with reverence and love."

1776 Commission · Trump
⚠ Strange Convergence

Neem discerns a paradox: both the far right and the far left have come to agree that American history is defined by whiteness — the left refusing assimilation into a tainted culture, the right viewing immigration as a threat to that same culture. The naturalized citizen — historically more patriotic than the native-born — is being excluded from both ends of the national narrative.

03 · The Collapse

How the Institutions Failed

The culture war didn't stay in academia. Tap each episode to read the full story.

In 1991, the George H. W. Bush administration asked a UCLA center to produce national history standards. When conservatives reviewed the guidelines — which reflected the latest scholarship — they were alarmed. Lynne Cheney denounced them for emphasizing failures over achievements. Rush Limbaugh said they aimed to teach students "that America is a rotten place."

The Senate voted to reject the standards 99–1. The message was unmistakable: no version of American history everyone could agree on was politically possible.

When the Bush administration designed No Child Left Behind, they knew history was too contentious for a standardized test. So they left it out entirely — holding schools accountable only for English and math.

The consequence: schools struggling on tested subjects cut social studies to compensate. Elementary-level social-studies time dropped by a third — 76 minutes per week, gone. High-school history became the safest place to park the football coach on a full salary. Within a decade, more than a third of social-studies educators were also coaching sports or teaching phys ed, relying heavily on textbooks and worksheets rather than primary sources.

Students joked that all their history teachers had the same first name: Coach.

Starting in the 1960s, as women and people of color entered the academy, scholars turned to class, race, and gender — giving voice to the voiceless, documenting injustice. This was a genuine enrichment of history.

But the pieces were never reassembled into a comprehensible national narrative. Scholars largely stopped writing single-volume histories of the United States. "People were suspicious of any national story as the handmaiden of ethnic nationalism," says Harvard historian Jill Lepore. Historians who had spent decades questioning patriotic myths awoke to discover they had ceded the task of storytelling to the likes of Bill O'Reilly and Newt Gingrich.

The Biden administration punted: it invited people to record personal histories on the theory that "your story is America's story" — individualism as a substitute for national narrative.

Trump returned in 2025 eager to leave his mark, but with no coherent vision beyond spectacle: a UFC fight on the South Lawn, a Formula 1 race in Washington D.C., a colossal archway near Arlington. His proposed National Garden of American Heroes — Davy Crockett, Julia Child, and Kobe Bryant — bears less resemblance to a national pantheon than to the setup of a bad joke.

Meanwhile, his administration ordered the National Park Service to remove signs that "inappropriately disparage" historical figures. A display on George Washington's slaves came down in Philadelphia; a Grand Canyon exhibit was stripped of its mention of tribal displacement. Deleting true accounts betrays a curious lack of confidence. Do the censors fear Americans will stop loving their country if they know the full story?

04 · Timeline

The Unraveling — A Chronology

1960s–70s
Academic revolution: women and minorities enter the academy; new scholarship on race, class, and gender explodes. Scholars stop writing single-volume national histories.
1991–1994
Bush 41 commissions national history standards from UCLA. Cheney denounces them; Senate rejects them 99–1. Federal consensus on teaching history collapses.
2001
No Child Left Behind omits history testing — not from indifference, but because everyone cares too much to agree. Schools begin defunding social studies.
2018
Jill Lepore publishes These Truths — the first sweeping single-volume national history in a generation. Massive popular response; readers say it makes them feel they belong.
2019
The 1619 Project reframes American history around slavery and 1619 rather than 1776. Ignites furious debate; draws both legitimate historical critique and political weaponization.
2021
Trump's 1776 Commission calls for approaching history "with reverence and love." State-level battles over critical race theory in schools intensify. Culture war goes local.
2025–2026
America turns 250. Trump's Semiquincentennial: UFC fight, F1 race, statues of Kobe Bryant. Park Service removes signs about slavery. Still no coherent national story.
05 · Signs of Hope

The Hunger for the Full Story

Appelbaum does not end in despair. Large majorities of Americans prefer history that challenges what they know over accounts that reinforce it. And voices from across the political spectrum are rediscovering the appeal — and the necessity — of a shared national narrative.

Researching why newly emancipated slaves did not leave the country that had so wronged them, Hannah-Jones found them arguing that — having finally gained rights and citizenship — they could not abandon the only land they had ever known. They resolved to stay and make the nation what it could and should have been.

"I actually did find that really inspiring," she told Appelbaum. "And I think that's the first time I ever felt I could see a path to patriotism." Conservatives frequently warn that studying dark chapters erodes patriotism. In Hannah-Jones's case, it had the opposite effect.

Her essay ends with her wish that she could tell her younger self to "boldly, proudly, draw the stars and those stripes of the American flag."

After Lepore published These Truths, she was astonished by readers who reached out to tell her what the book meant to them. One woman wrote to say she had bought it to study for her citizenship exam but, enthralled, kept reading.

"I really feel like I belong here now, because I understand the whole journey that is the story of this land and this people and these ideas and this country."

Most significantly, Lepore found that readers wanted the full story — the progress and the revanchism, the beauty and the ugliness, the racial massacres and the Indian New Deal. Americans are not fragile. They can handle — and hunger for — the whole truth.

Large majorities of Americans prefer history that challenges what they know over accounts that reinforce it. By an almost nine-to-one margin, they think it's at least as important to learn about other racial and ethnic communities as about their own.

They overwhelmingly agree on the importance of learning about past harms, even when doing so makes them uncomfortable. After seven years on the road with the 1619 Project, Hannah-Jones says: "I've never had someone walk away and say they hate this country. They say they're ashamed of things this country has done, that they're deeply disappointed, and they want to see the country be better."
